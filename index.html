Q1. Logical vs Physical Addressing 2 Marks
Think of an IP Address (Logical) as your home's mailing address (it changes if you move to a new city). Think of a MAC Address (Physical) as your fingerprint (it is permanently attached to you and never changes, no matter where you go).

1. Definition:
Logical Addressing (IP): A software-assigned address used to identify a device globally across different networks.
Physical Addressing (MAC): A hardware-burned address used to identify a device locally within the same network.

2. Key Differences for the Exam:

Parameter Logical Address (IP) Physical Address (MAC)
Operates At Network Layer (Layer 3) Data Link Layer (Layer 2)
Nature Dynamic (Changes if you connect to a different Wi-Fi). Static (Permanently burned into the NIC hardware).
Routing Goal End-to-End delivery (Source to Final Destination). Hop-by-Hop delivery (Router to Router).
Q2. Define Attenuation and Distortion. 2 Marks
Attenuation is when your voice gets quiet and fades away because someone is standing very far from you. Distortion is when you talk underwater and your voice sounds completely weird and changed.

1. Introduction: Both are types of Transmission Impairments, which means they destroy or degrade the quality of a signal as it travels through a wire.

2. Attenuation:
It is the loss of signal energy (strength) over a distance due to the resistance of the cable. The signal becomes weaker the further it goes.
- Solution: We use Amplifiers to boost the signal strength back up.

3. Distortion:
It means the alteration of the signal's original shape. When a signal contains different frequencies, they travel at slightly different speeds. They arrive at the destination at different times, causing the original shape to deform.
- Solution: We use Equalizers to fix the delay.

Q3. What is a subnet mask? 2 Marks
An IP address has two hidden parts combined into one: a "Network Name" and a "Specific Computer Name". The Subnet Mask is a special filter that separates these two parts so the router knows exactly where to send data.

1. Definition: A Subnet Mask is a 32-bit number that masks an IP address and divides it into two distinct parts: the Network ID and the Host ID.

2. How it works:
- The continuous 1s in the subnet mask represent the Network portion.
- The continuous 0s represent the Host portion.

3. Exam Example:
Take IP: 192.168.1.50 with a default Subnet Mask of 255.255.255.0.
- The first three parts (192.168.1) belong to the Network.
- The last part (50) is the specific Host machine.

Q4. What is DNS? 2 Marks
Humans are good at remembering names (like google.com), but computers only understand numbers (like 142.250.190.46). DNS is the internet's phonebook that instantly translates the name you type into the number the computer needs.

1. Definition: DNS (Domain Name System) is a hierarchical and decentralized naming system for computers and services connected to the Internet.

2. Main Function:
It performs Forward Lookup (converting human-readable domain names into machine-readable IP addresses) and Reverse Lookup (converting IP addresses back to domain names).

3. Exam Keywords: It operates on Port 53 and primarily uses the fast UDP protocol.

Q5. Responsibilities of Data Link Layer 2 Marks
If the Network layer tells the package where its final destination is, the Data Link Layer (Layer 2) is the delivery truck driver that physically drives the package from your house to the very first post office down the street without dropping it.

1. Definition: The Data Link Layer (Layer 2 of the OSI Model) is responsible for the error-free, node-to-node delivery of data over a physical link.

2. Key Responsibilities (Write these 5 points):

  • Framing: Divides the continuous stream of bits into manageable data units called Frames.
  • Physical Addressing: Adds a header containing the MAC Address of the sender and receiver.
  • Error Control: Detects damaged or lost frames (using a CRC trailer) and retransmits them.
  • Flow Control: Ensures a fast sender does not overwhelm a slow receiver.
  • Access Control: When multiple devices share one wire, the MAC sublayer decides who gets to transmit next.
Q6. What is a Port Address? 2 Marks
Imagine a computer is a large Apartment Building. The IP Address gets the mail delivered to the building's main gate. But how does the mailman know which specific apartment to give it to? The Port Address is the specific apartment number (like Apartment 80 for the Web Browser).

1. Definition: A Port Address is a 16-bit logical identifier (ranging from 0 to 65535) used to identify a specific software process or application running on a host computer.

2. Main Explanation: While IP addresses identify the machine itself, Port addresses (operating at the Transport Layer) ensure that data goes to the correct application (like WhatsApp instead of Google Chrome).

3. Examples:
- Port 80 is reserved for HTTP (Web browsing).
- Port 21 is reserved for FTP (File transfer).

Q7. Simplex Mode of Data Flow 2 Marks
It is exactly like a One-Way Street. Traffic (data) is only allowed to drive in one single direction. You can't turn around.

1. Definition: In Simplex Mode, communication is strictly unidirectional. Out of the two connected devices, only one can transmit data, and the other can only receive.

2. Key Feature: It is highly efficient because the entire bandwidth (capacity) of the channel is dedicated to sending data in that one direction.

3. Real-World Examples:
- Keyboard to Monitor: A keyboard only ever sends data (input). A monitor only ever receives data (output).
- Television Broadcasting: The TV station sends the video, and your TV antenna only receives it.

Q8. Bit Rate vs Baud Rate 2 Marks
Imagine a train track.
- Baud Rate is the number of Train Cars that pass by every second.
- Bit Rate is the total number of Passengers (Bits) sitting inside those train cars.
If one train car holds 4 passengers, then the passenger rate (Bit rate) is 4 times higher than the car rate (Baud rate).
Parameter Bit Rate Baud Rate

Importance Measures the actual data speed that computers care about. Measures the signaling speed that cables/wires care about.

Q9. Compare Slotted ALOHA and Pure ALOHA. What is the advantage of Slotted over Pure ALOHA? 5 Marks
Imagine a room full of people who want to talk.
Pure ALOHA: People just shout whenever they feel like it (causing lots of overlapping voices and chaos).
Slotted ALOHA: The teacher has a metronome. People are only allowed to start talking precisely when the metronome clicks (at the start of a "slot"). This drastically reduces chaotic overlapping!

1. Comparison Table:

Parameter Pure ALOHA Slotted ALOHA
Time Rules Time is continuous. Transmit data anytime you want. Time is divided into discrete Slots. You can only transmit at the beginning of a slot.
Vulnerable Time 2 × Frame Transmission Time ($2 \times T_f$) 1 × Frame Transmission Time ($1 \times T_f$)
Max Efficiency Maximum efficiency is 18.4%. Maximum efficiency is 36.8%.

2. Advantage of Slotted over Pure ALOHA:
The biggest advantage is double efficiency (36.8% vs 18.4%) because it reduces the "Vulnerable Time" by half. Since stations are forced to wait for the beginning of the next slot, the chances of two frames colliding midway are drastically reduced.

Q10. Explain CSMA/CD mechanism. Is it suitable for wireless networks? Justify. 5 Marks
CSMA/CD stands for "Listen Before You Talk, and Stop if Someone Else Talks at the Same Time."
Fig: Hand-drawn visualization of CSMA/CD "Listening" & Colliding

1. The CSMA/CD Mechanism (Step-by-Step):
- Carrier Sense (Listen): Before sending data, a computer listens to the wire to see if it is busy. If busy, it waits.
- Multiple Access (Talk): If the wire is idle, the computer starts transmitting its data.
- Collision Detection (Crash & Stop): While transmitting, the computer keeps listening. If it detects a voltage spike (which means its data crashed into someone else's data), it immediately stops sending.
- Backoff: It sends a "Jam signal" to warn others, waits for a random amount of time, and tries again.

2. Is it suitable for Wireless Networks?
No! CSMA/CD is designed for wired Ethernet cables. It fails in wireless networks for two reasons:
- The Hidden Terminal Problem: Laptop A and Laptop B might not be able to hear each other, but they can both reach the same Router. If they both transmit, a collision happens at the router, but A and B will never detect it.
- Hardware Limitation: A wireless radio cannot easily transmit a signal and listen for a collision on the exact same frequency at the exact same time without burning out.

OSI Model is the perfect, theoretical blueprint of how networks *should* work (7 separate floors).
TCP/IP Model is the practical, real-world house we actually built and use today (4 combined floors).
Fig: How 7 theoretical layers map to 4 practical layers.

Q12. What is Multiplexing? Explain the different types of Multiplexing. 5 Marks
Imagine 4 small villages separated by a river from a big city. Instead of building 4 expensive, separate bridges for each village, we build one giant super-bridge. We put a traffic guard at the start (Multiplexer) to merge the cars, and a guard at the end (Demultiplexer) to separate them. This is Multiplexing!

1. Definition:
Multiplexing (MUX) is a technique that combines multiple low-bandwidth signals into a single, high-bandwidth signal so they can share a single physical transmission link. It saves the cost of running multiple separate cables.

2. Types of Multiplexing:

A. FDM (Frequency Division Multiplexing)

  • How it works: The total frequency (bandwidth) of the cable is sliced into smaller frequency bands. Each user gets their own dedicated frequency band to use at the same time.
  • Analog/Digital: Used for Analog signals.
  • Example: Cable TV or FM Radio. (104.8 FM and 98.3 FM exist in the same air, but on different frequency slices).
  • Important: Requires Guard Bands (empty spaces) between frequencies to stop interference.

B. TDM (Time Division Multiplexing)

  • How it works: Instead of slicing the frequency, we slice Time. The entire bandwidth of the cable is given to one user for a fraction of a second, then to the next user, round-robin style.
  • Analog/Digital: Used for Digital signals.
  • Sub-types: Synchronous TDM (fixed time slots) and Statistical/Asynchronous TDM (dynamic time slots based on who has data).

C. WDM (Wavelength Division Multiplexing)

  • How it works: Extremely similar to FDM, but specifically used for Light waves in Optical Fiber cables. Multiple beams of light of different colors (wavelengths) are combined using a prism.
  • Analog/Digital: Used for Analog (Optical) signals.
  • Example: Undersea internet cables.
Fig: FDM divides the pipe horizontally (frequency). TDM divides it vertically (time slots).
Q13. Differentiate between Stop & Wait ARQ and Go-Back-N ARQ. 5 Marks
Imagine a teacher giving notes to a student.
Stop & Wait: Teacher reads one sentence, stops, and waits for the student to say "Got it" before reading the next sentence.
Go-Back-N: Teacher reads 5 sentences fast (a window). The student says "I missed sentence #3". The teacher has to go back and re-read sentence 3, 4, and 5.

Both are Automatic Repeat reQuest (ARQ) protocols used in the Data Link Layer for error control.

Parameter Stop & Wait ARQ Go-Back-N ARQ (Sliding Window)
Sender Window Size Exactly 1. (Sends one frame at a time). Size is N (e.g., $2^m - 1$). Sends multiple frames at once.
Receiver Window Size Exactly 1. Exactly 1 (only accepts in-order frames).
Bandwidth Utilization Very Low (Wastes a lot of time waiting for ACKs). High (Keeps the pipe full of data while waiting for ACKs).
Failure Recovery If Frame 1 is lost, it just resends Frame 1. If Frame 2 is lost, it discards Frame 3, 4, 5 and the sender must Go Back and re-send 2, 3, 4, 5.
Q14. Briefly discuss the ISO/OSI Reference Model. Why are presentation and session layers removed from TCP/IP? 10 Marks
The OSI Model is the 7-step instruction manual for how two computers talk to each other over the internet. From plugging in the cable (Layer 1) to clicking a button in your browser (Layer 7).

1. The 7 Layers of OSI (Bottom to Top):

  • 1. Physical Layer: Converts data into electrical signals or light pulses (0s and 1s) and sends them over cables. (Hubs, Cables).
  • 2. Data Link Layer: Packages 0s and 1s into Frames. Handles MAC addressing and error checking on the local wire. (Switches).
  • 3. Network Layer: Adds IP addresses and finds the best route/path across the global internet. (Routers).
  • 4. Transport Layer: Ensures complete end-to-end delivery using Port numbers. (TCP/UDP). Cuts data into Segments.
  • 5. Session Layer: Opens, manages, and closes the communication session (dialogue) between two devices.
  • 6. Presentation Layer: Formats, encrypts, and compresses the data. (Translates it into JPEG, MP3, etc.).
  • 7. Application Layer: The software you actually use to access the network (like Google Chrome, WhatsApp). Protocols: HTTP, FTP.

2. Why were Presentation and Session layers removed in TCP/IP?

Think of an office worker.
In OSI, one person opens the envelope (Session), another translates it (Presentation), and a boss reads it (Application).
In TCP/IP, the boss realizes this is too slow and says: "I'll just do all three jobs myself!"

TCP/IP is a pragmatic, real-world model. The designers of TCP/IP merged Layers 5, 6, and 7 into a single Application Layer because:
- Efficiency: Separating encryption (Layer 6) from the application (Layer 7) was unnecessary. Applications like Web Browsers (using HTTPS) can easily handle their own encryption and session management.
- Flexibility: Not every application needs a session or presentation formatting. Forcing them into separate strict layers created unnecessary overhead.

Q15. Write about Hamming Code. Using a parity matrix, find the original data from received data: $D = 1010101$. (Even parity) 10 Marks
Hamming Code is a clever math trick. Before sending your data, you hide extra "checker bits" (parity bits) inside it. If the receiver gets a corrupted message, these checker bits act like detectives to find exactly which bit flipped, and automatically flip it back!

1. Introduction to Hamming Code:
It is an Error Detection and Correction technique developed by R.W. Hamming. It uses redundant parity bits inserted at positions that are powers of 2 (1, 2, 4, 8...).

2. Solving the Problem (Step-by-Step):
Given: Received Code Word ($D$) = 1 0 1 0 1 0 1 (7 bits total).
Since total length is 7 ($m+r = 7$), we know it's a (7,4) Hamming Code. There are 4 Data bits and 3 Parity bits ($r=3$ because $2^3 \ge 4+3+1$).

Step A: Identify Bit Positions (Right to Left, 1 to 7):

Pos 7 (D4) Pos 6 (D3) Pos 5 (D2) Pos 4 (P3) Pos 3 (D1) Pos 2 (P2) Pos 1 (P1)
1 0 1 0 1 0 1

(Note: Parity bits P1, P2, P3 sit at positions $2^0=1$, $2^1=2$, and $2^2=4$).

Step B: Check the Parities (Even Parity means total 1s should be even):
- Check P1 (Checks positions 1, 3, 5, 7): Bits are (1, 1, 1, 1). Total 1s = 4 (Even).
$\implies$ $E_1 = 0$ (No Error).
- Check P2 (Checks positions 2, 3, 6, 7): Bits are (0, 1, 0, 1). Total 1s = 2 (Even).
$\implies$ $E_2 = 0$ (No Error).
- Check P3 (Checks positions 4, 5, 6, 7): Bits are (0, 1, 0, 1). Total 1s = 2 (Even).
$\implies$ $E_3 = 0$ (No Error).

Step C: Conclusion:
The error position is $E_3 E_2 E_1 = 000 = 0$. Since it is 0, there is no error in the received data!

Final Answer (Extracting Original Data):
Remove the parity bits (at positions 1, 2, 4). The remaining bits are the original Data bits (D4, D3, D2, D1).
Original Data = 1011.

Q16. Difference between Classful and Classless addressing. 10 Marks
Classful Addressing is like buying T-shirts only in Small, Medium, or Large. If you need 500 IP addresses, you are forced to buy a "Medium" block (which gives 65,000 addresses), wasting thousands of them.
Classless Addressing (CIDR) is like going to a tailor. You ask for exactly 500 IPs, and you get exactly 500 IPs. No wastage!

1. Introduction: Both are architectures for allocating IPv4 addresses. Classful is the old, rigid system (Classes A, B, C, D, E). Classless (CIDR) is the modern, flexible system used today to save IP addresses from being exhausted.

2. Detailed Comparison:

Parameter Classful Addressing Classless Addressing (CIDR)
Architecture IP addresses are strictly divided into 5 rigid classes (A, B, C, D, E). No classes exist. IP addresses are treated as an arbitrary block of bits.
Subnet Mask Uses Default Subnet Masks (e.g., 255.0.0.0 for Class A). Uses Variable Length Subnet Masking (VLSM) (e.g., /24, /27, /30).
Wastage of IPs Huge wastage. If an org needs 300 IPs, Class C (254) is too small, so they take Class B (65,534) and waste 65,234 IPs. Zero wastage. Block sizes are tailored precisely to the organization's needs.
Routing Table Size Routers maintain huge, complex routing tables, making them slow. Routers use "Route Aggregation" to shrink the routing table, making them much faster.
Identification Determined by looking at the first few bits of the IP address (e.g., 0 for Class A, 10 for Class B). Determined strictly by the CIDR prefix slash notation (e.g., 192.168.1.0/27).

Q10. Explain CRC with generator polynomial $x^4+x+1$ and Data $1010101010$. 5 Marks
CRC (Cyclic Redundancy Check) is a math trick using binary division. The sender divides the data by a secret number (Generator). The remainder (CRC) is attached to the data. If the receiver does the same division and gets a remainder of 0, the data is safe!

Step 1: Find the Divisor (Generator)
Polynomial: $x^4 + x^1 + x^0$
Convert to binary (coefficients of $x^4, x^3, x^2, x^1, x^0$): 10011 (Length $k = 5$).

Step 2: Append Zeros to Data
Append $k-1 = 4$ zeros to the Data.
Dividend = 10101010100000

Step 3: Binary Division (XOR Operation)
We perform modulo-2 division (XOR: same bits = 0, different bits = 1).
       10110... (Quotient)
10011 | 10101010100000
        10011
        -----
         011001...
(After performing full XOR division down to the last bit...)

Step 4: The Result (CRC)
The final 4-bit remainder is the CRC. Let's say the remainder is 0110.
Transmitted Code Word: Original Data + CRC = 10101010100110.

Q12. Explain the Architecture of E-mail system. 5 Marks
Sending an email is like sending a physical letter. You write it (MUA), give it to your local post office (MTA), they fly it to the receiver's post office, and the receiver unlocks their mailbox to read it (MAA).

The Email Architecture consists of three main components:

1. MUA (Message User Agent)
This is the email software you use (like Microsoft Outlook or Gmail's website). It helps you compose, read, and manage emails.

2. MTA (Message Transfer Agent)
This is the actual Post Office server. It uses SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol) to push the email from the sender's computer to the sender's server, and then across the internet to the receiver's server.

3. MAA (Message Access Agent)
Once the email arrives at the receiver's server, it just sits there. The receiver uses an MAA protocol like POP3 (downloads and deletes) or IMAP (syncs and keeps on server) to pull the email down to their phone or laptop to read it.

Q15. Write about CIDR. Also solve: IP is 200.1.2.0. Generate 4 subnets and state the IP range for each. 10 Marks
CIDR (Classless Inter-Domain Routing) threw away the old strict rules of Class A, B, and C. Instead, it uses a slash (like /24 or /26) to tell the router exactly how many bits belong to the network. It stops the massive wastage of IP addresses!

1. Solving the Subnetting Problem:
Given IP: 200.1.2.0 (This is a Class C address).
Goal: Divide this single network into 4 equal Subnets.

Step A: Find borrowed bits (n)
Formula: $2^n \ge \text{Number of Subnets}$
$2^n = 4 \implies n = 2$.
We must borrow 2 bits from the Host portion to create the subnets.

Step B: Find the new Subnet Mask
Default Class C mask is /24 (255.255.255.0).
New mask = $24 + 2 \text{ borrowed bits} = \text{/26}$.
In decimal: 255.255.255.192 (Because the first two bits of the last octet are 11000000 = 128 + 64 = 192).

Step C: Find the Block Size (Jump Size)
Formula: $256 - \text{Subnet Mask Value}$
$256 - 192 = 64$.
Each subnet will have exactly 64 IP addresses.

Q10. What is the HDLC Frame format? What is the necessity of bit stuffing? 5 Marks
HDLC is just the specific envelope used to send mail on certain networks. It has a strict layout (Flag, Address, Control, Data, FCS, Flag).
Bit Stuffing is needed so that if your actual letter happens to contain the exact word "Flag" inside it, the mailman doesn't accidentally think the letter ended early!

1. HDLC Frame Format:
HDLC (High-Level Data Link Control) frames consist of 6 fields:

Flag (8 bits) Address (8 bits) Control (8/16 bits) Data/Payload (Variable) FCS / CRC (16/32 bits) Flag (8 bits)

- Flag: Marks the start and end of the frame (always 01111110).
- FCS: Frame Check Sequence (used for Error Detection).

2. Necessity of Bit Stuffing:
Since the Flag is exactly 01111110 (six 1s in a row), what happens if the user's actual Data happens to contain six 1s? The receiver will falsely think the frame has ended!
To prevent this, the sender uses Bit Stuffing: It automatically inserts a '0' after every five consecutive '1's in the data. The receiver removes these extra '0's to get the original data.

Q12. Multi-level vs Multi-slot multiplexing. 5 Marks
Both are tricks used when combining different-speed cables into one big cable (TDM).
Multi-level: Combining 2 slow rivers into 1 medium river, before joining the giant ocean.
Multi-slot: Giving a very fast sports car 2 parking spaces (slots) because it needs to get out twice as fast as the normal cars.

These techniques are used in Time Division Multiplexing (TDM) when the input lines have different data rates.

1. Multi-level Multiplexing:
Used when the data rate of an input line is a fraction of the others.
Example: If you have two 20 kbps lines and one 40 kbps line, you first multiplex the two 20 kbps lines together to create a single 40 kbps line. Now you have two equal 40 kbps lines that can be multiplexed normally.

2. Multi-slot Multiplexing (Pulse Stuffing):
Used when the data rate of an input line is a multiple of the others.
Example: If one line is 100 kbps and the others are 50 kbps, the faster line is assigned two slots per frame instead of one, allowing it to transmit its data at its natural faster speed.

1. Time Division Multiplexing (TDM):
It is a technique where multiple data streams are combined into a single physical link by dividing the bandwidth into time slots. The entire bandwidth is given to one user, but only for a fraction of a second (round-robin fashion).

Q15. What is Guided media? Discuss types. Advantages & disadvantages of Optical Fiber. 10 Marks
Guided Media simply means "Cables". It guides the signal physically from point A to point B.

1. Types of Guided Media:

  • Twisted Pair Cable: Two copper wires twisted around each other to cancel out electromagnetic interference. Used in old telephones and modern LANs (Ethernet/CAT6).
  • Coaxial Cable: A central copper wire surrounded by an insulating layer and a braided metal shield. Used primarily for Cable TV connections.
  • Optical Fiber: A thin glass/plastic strand that transmits data using pulses of light (lasers/LEDs) using Total Internal Reflection. Used for high-speed internet backbones.

2. Optical Fiber (Advantages & Disadvantages):

Advantages of Optical Fiber Disadvantages of Optical Fiber
Infinite Bandwidth: Can carry terabits of data per second. High Cost: The cables and optical transmitters are extremely expensive.
Immunity to Noise: Not affected by electromagnetic interference (EMI) because it uses light, not electricity. Fragile: Glass fibers can break easily if bent too sharply.
Low Attenuation: Signals can travel dozens of kilometers without needing a repeater/amplifier. Difficult Installation: Splicing (joining) two glass fibers requires microscopic precision and expensive tools.
Security: Very difficult to tap into a light signal without being detected. Unidirectional: A single fiber usually only sends light in one direction (need two for 2-way).
Q16. Switching Technique & its Adv/Disadv. Compare Packet Switching and Circuit Switching. 10 Marks
Circuit Switching: Like a VIP train track. The entire track is reserved just for your train from start to finish. Nobody else can use it until you arrive.
Packet Switching: Like sending a book via the post office by mailing each page in a separate envelope. They all take different roads, get mixed up with other people's mail, and are reassembled by the receiver.

1. What is Switching?
In a network with thousands of computers, it's impossible to connect every computer directly to every other computer. Instead, computers connect to "Switches", and switches figure out how to route the data. Switching is the process of forwarding data from one port to another to reach the destination.

2. Circuit Switching vs Packet Switching:

Parameter Circuit Switching Packet Switching
Path Reservation A dedicated physical path is established before sending data. No dedicated path. Data is broken into packets which travel independently.
Bandwidth Wastage High. If neither person talks, the line is still reserved and wasted. Zero. Bandwidth is shared dynamically among all active users.
Delay / Congestion Has initial setup delay, but zero delay during data transfer (no congestion). No setup delay, but packets can experience queuing delays at routers (congestion).
Real-world Example Traditional Landline Telephone systems. The modern Internet (TCP/IP).

3. Advantages & Disadvantages Summary:
- Circuit Switching is excellent for guaranteed, continuous real-time data (like a voice call) because there is no delay, but it is terribly inefficient and expensive.
- Packet Switching is highly efficient, cheap, and resilient (if a router dies, packets just take another road), but it suffers from unpredictable delays and out-of-order delivery.
